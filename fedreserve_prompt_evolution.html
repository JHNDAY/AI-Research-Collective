An Anatomy of a Prompt for:
Federal Reserve Monetary Policy

From verdict to inquiry — five iterations toward a balanced, evidence-seeking prompt

A single prompt was drafted, reviewed, and revised five times in a live coaching session. Each iteration added one new dimension of craft: removing bias, naming focal indicators, balancing perspectives, and finally requesting a visualization artifact. This page traces every step of that evolution.

Version 1 — The Verdict Draft

I want to explore the poor policy decisions on the part of the federal reserve. specifically in monetary policy. I am particularily interested in money supply, inflation, and unemployment. Although the Fed is frequently composed of respected econmic professionals they seem to get it wrong far too many times.
✦ What's working The tension embedded in "respected economic professionals… get it wrong" is genuinely compelling — it sets up a real intellectual problem. Naming three specific indicators (money supply, inflation, unemployment) rather than just "the economy" gives Claude meaningful scope.
→ What's still missing The prompt opens with a verdict ("poor policy decisions") before asking a question. Claude is being invited to confirm a conclusion, not pursue an inquiry. There is no indication of what a successful response looks like, and no personal context grounding the curiosity.
↓
+ Open-ended framing replaces verdict language

Version 2 — The Inquiry Opens

Subject Identity Purpose Audience Success Criterion Intellectual Frame
I want to explore the poor policy decisions on the part of the federal reserve. specifically in monetary policy. I am particularily interested in money supply, inflation, and unemployment. Although the Fed is frequently composed of respected econmic professionals they seem to get it wrong far too many times.

I want to examine Federal Reserve monetary policy — particularly around money supply, inflation, and unemployment — and understand where critics say it has fallen short, where defenders say the constraints made better outcomes impossible, and what the evidence actually shows.
✦ What's working The three-part closing structure — critics / defenders / evidence — is a genuine structural achievement. It builds fairness into the prompt's architecture. The phrase "what the evidence actually shows" signals intellectual honesty and will prompt Claude to bring data rather than opinion.
→ What's still missing The prompt now has two voices that conflict: the opening paragraph still delivers a verdict, while the second paragraph opens the inquiry. The tension between them will pull Claude's response in two directions. One voice needs to give way.
↓
+ Remove "poor" — let the inquiry lead

→ What's still missing A broken sentence remains ("I specifically in monetary policy" — a cutting accident). More importantly, the fourth sentence still carries verdict-tone: "they seem to get it wrong far too many times." It is the one remaining thing holding the prompt back from full coherence.

The three-part closing structure is clean and rigorous. It reads like someone who has thought carefully about how inquiry works.
→ What's still missing The verdict sentence ("they seem to get it wrong far too many times") still appears and still conflicts with the balanced framing of the second paragraph. The prompt is one sentence away from full coherence. A visualization artifact request would complete the intent.

Version 5 — The Final Prompt

Subject Identity Purpose Audience Success Criterion Intellectual Frame
I want to explore the policy decisions on the part of the federal reserve. I am specifically interested in monetary policy. I am particularily interested in money supply, inflation, and unemployment. Although the Fed is frequently composed of respected econmic professionals they seem to get it wrong far too many times.

I want to examine Federal Reserve monetary policy — particularly around money supply, inflation, and unemployment — and understand where critics say it has fallen short, where defenders say the constraints made better outcomes impossible, and what the evidence actually shows.

Please create an HTML file artifact that visualizes your response to this narrative — don't create any API calls in the artifact.
✦ What's working The explicit artifact request — "visualizes your response… don't create any API calls" — is precise and technically informed. It tells Claude both what to produce and what constraint to observe. The full arc of the prompt (exploration → focus → balance → output format) is now complete.
→ What's still missing The one remaining sentence to consider before final use: "they seem to get it wrong far too many times" still carries verdict-tone. For a Current Events session where balance matters most, retiring that line would make this prompt exemplary. The visualization will let the evidence speak for itself.

◈ The Coaching Principle

The most important move in this session was the shift from verdict to inquiry. A prompt that opens with a conclusion ("poor policy decisions") invites Claude to perform agreement. A prompt that opens with genuine curiosity — naming both the critics and the defenders, then asking what the evidence shows — invites Claude to think. The technical artifact request at the end was the final maturation: it transformed a conversation into a deliverable. The one remaining opportunity is personal context: why does this writer care about Fed policy? Lived experience of inflation, unemployment, or financial crisis would make the inquiry richer still — and would help Claude calibrate not just what to say, but who it is speaking to.

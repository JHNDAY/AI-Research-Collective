Generative AI & RAG — Harvest · Repository · Mine
Two architectures that solve the same problem — and how each one works

📖 Meaning of Words Foundation

▼

AI processes meaning at three levels simultaneously. The word "dog" illustrates how each level works — and what it means for your prompts.

Level 1 — The Concrete Instance

"Dog" at its most literal: a four-legged domesticated animal. AI recognizes the dictionary definition — the denotative anchor.
For your prompts: specific terms like "Keynesian economics," "Meiji Restoration," or "DNA double helix" give AI an unambiguous starting point. Vague phrases like "the economics thing" force it to guess.

Level 2 — Contextual Transformation

"You sly dog!" — no animal in sight. Surrounding words ("sly," the exclamation, the phrase structure) have completely shifted the meaning.
For your prompts: context shapes every word you use. "Revolution" means something different in 1789 France, 1917 Russia, and a discussion of scientific paradigm shifts. Framing details — audience, focus, angle — transform how AI interprets your entire question.

Level 3 — The Vast Corpus of Patterns

AI has no dictionary entry for "dog." Instead it learned statistical patterns across billions of sentences: "dog" near "bark" and "leash" (animal), near "sly" and "cunning" (metaphor), in "hot dog" near "mustard" (food), in "underdog" near "unlikely victory" (status).
For your prompts: when you use a word, AI activates all its learned patterns and uses your surrounding context to select the right ones — which is why even a rambling, "messy" question works.

Written words often contain much more information than meets the eye — and AI is reading at all three levels at once.

✍️ Basic Generative AI: The Foundation Concept

▼

Your thinking partner for discussing, interpreting, and assessing ideas — drawing on vast knowledge compressed into trained weights to help you explore a topic, weigh arguments, and sharpen your own thinking.

▶️ See How it Works

When you want to think through a topic — explore its tensions, weigh competing interpretations, draft discussion questions, or assess the strength of an argument — generative AI is a brilliant, tireless interlocutor. During training it absorbed an enormous breadth of knowledge, compressing billions of associations into mathematical weights. Your prompt activates the relevant patterns in those weights; generation then proceeds token by token from that activation. No documents are retrieved. No search is performed. The "library" was dissolved into cognition at training time — vast and richly interconnected, but frozen at a cutoff date and unable to cite a specific source.

Think of it as a scholar who has read everything and absorbed it all — you ask a question, and they draw on deep, integrated knowledge without pausing to search. They cannot hand you the source page; the knowledge has become part of how they think.

Example — Generative AI as thinking partner

"What are the strongest arguments for and against crediting Rosalind Franklin as a co-discoverer of the DNA double helix?" — the AI engages the intellectual and ethical dimensions, weighs the evidence, and helps you think through the question, drawing on compressed training associations.

Where Generative AI reaches its limit

"What does the latest scholarship say about Franklin's role, and where can I read more?" — for that question, you need RAG. Generative AI thinks with you from what it already knows; RAG harvests specific, current, citable sources and generates from them.

⚡ RAG: Harvest · Repository · Mine Concept

▼

RAG solves the same problem as generative AI — how to respond usefully to your question — but by a different route: it first harvests relevant passages from a document index, assembles them into a targeted repository in the context window, then mines that repository using the same token-by-token generation process.

The three phases are distinct and sequential — retrieval happens before generation begins, not during it:

Phase 1 — Harvest

Your prompt is encoded as a vector and matched against pre-indexed documents — uploaded readings, web pages, transcripts, syllabi. The most relevant passages are selected by similarity. Not everything: only what the search judges pertinent. Like a student surveying the library stacks and pulling the relevant volumes.

Phase 2 — Repository

The harvested passages are assembled into the context window alongside your original prompt. This is the "spreading sources on the desk" step. The language model has not yet begun to write — it simply now has specific, curated material in front of it. The sources remain intact and citable.

Phase 3 — Mine

Generation proceeds token by token, exactly as in basic generative AI — but now conditioned on both your prompt and the harvested repository. The response is grounded in specific documents and can cite page references or links precisely. The same writing process; a different desk.

Example — RAG applied to course preparation

"Based on this week's assigned reading on Rosalind Franklin, what three discussion questions would probe the most contested interpretations?" — a RAG system harvests the relevant passages from the uploaded text, assembles them as context, and mines them to craft questions grounded in specific arguments and evidence, with page references.

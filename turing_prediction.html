Chapter I

The Wager: What Turing Predicted in 1950

In the closing pages of his landmark 1950 paper, Alan Turing set aside philosophical abstraction and made a concrete, falsifiable prediction. He staked a claim not on the metaphysics of mind, but on observable behaviour — and gave it a timeline.

"I believe that in about fifty years' time it will be possible to programme computers, with a storage capacity of about 10⁹, to make them play the imitation game so well that an average interrogator will not have more than 70 per cent chance of making the right identification after five minutes of questioning." — A.M. Turing, "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," Mind, 1950

Three distinct claims are embedded here: a timeline (fifty years, placing the target at roughly 2000), a hardware threshold (10⁹ units of storage), and a behavioural benchmark (≤70% interrogator accuracy after five minutes). Each would be tested differently — and the results were instructive.

~2000 Target Year

Fifty years from 1950. The millennium as deadline.

10⁹ Storage Threshold

One billion bits — roughly 125 megabytes in modern terms.

≤70% Interrogator Accuracy

An average human judge, five minutes of questioning.

Turing then made a second, softer prediction: that by century's end, educated opinion would shift so that speaking of machines "thinking" would no longer invite contradiction. He was replacing a philosophical question with a sociological one — meaning is use, and use would change.

Chapter II

The Storage Reality: From 10⁹ to 10²³

Turing's hardware threshold — 10⁹ bits, or approximately 125 megabytes — was crossed without ceremony sometime in the mid-1980s. Consumer computers had achieved it before the decade was out. What followed was an escalation of storage capacity beyond anything his 1950 imagination could have encompassed.

Modern frontier language models carry roughly 30,000 times Turing's entire storage threshold in their weights alone. The training data that shaped those weights represents 10¹⁵ bits — a million times his benchmark. Global digital storage exceeds it by a factor of 100 trillion.

And yet — crossing 10⁹ bits in the 1980s produced no intelligent machine. Crossing 10¹² in the 1990s produced no intelligent machine. The storage threshold was necessary but nowhere near sufficient. Turing had misidentified the bottleneck.

Chapter III

The Failed Path: Ever More Sophisticated Programs

Turing imagined the route to machine intelligence as essentially one of programming — of encoding knowledge, rules, and reasoning procedures explicitly into a sufficiently large computer. For five decades, the field of artificial intelligence followed precisely this path. The results were impressive, brittle, and ultimately inadequate.

1956

Logic Theorist & the Dartmouth Conference

Newell, Shaw and Simon demonstrate machine theorem-proving. McCarthy coins "Artificial Intelligence." The founding premise: intelligence is symbol manipulation, and symbols can be programmed.

1965

ELIZA — The First Conversational Illusion

Weizenbaum's pattern-matching program fools users into believing it understands. A warning sign: the appearance of intelligence and its substance are easily separated. ELIZA has no knowledge, only rules.

1970s

Expert Systems — Intelligence as Rule Codification

MYCIN, DENDRAL, Xcon: human expertise hand-encoded into thousands of IF-THEN rules. Worked in narrow domains. Collapsed under the weight of exceptions, edge cases, and the combinatorial explosion of real-world ambiguity.

1984

Cyc — The Grand Encyclopaedic Project

Lenat's project to hand-encode all human common sense — millions of axioms over decades. Still ongoing. Never achieved general reasoning. Demonstrated the futility of trying to enumerate what humans know implicitly.

1980s–90s

The AI Winters

Repeated cycles of overpromising and underdelivering. DARPA funding collapses. The Lighthill Report in the UK. Turing's fifty-year deadline of 2000 approaches with no credible candidate for the imitation game.

2000

Turing's Deadline — Quietly Missed

The millennium passes. Storage has exceeded 10⁹ bits by many orders of magnitude. No program exists that could plausibly confuse an attentive interrogator for five minutes. The programmatic approach has reached its ceiling.

Verdict on the Programmatic Path

The fundamental error was ontological. Human language and thought do not operate by explicit rules — they operate through statistical intuition built from immense exposure. You cannot program what was never articulated. Turing's vision of a "well-programmed" machine was, in retrospect, the wrong model entirely.

Chapter IV

The Dumb but Brilliant Solution: Neural Networks

The approach that finally achieved Turing's benchmark shares almost nothing with what Turing imagined. There are no rules. No knowledge representations. No logical inference. Instead: billions of numerical weights, adjusted by gradient descent through exposure to vast quantities of human text. It is, in one sense, profoundly stupid. In another, it is the most powerful cognitive tool ever built.

"Dumb but Brilliant"

No programmer encodes language knowledge. No expert distils their understanding into rules. The system is exposed to human text at massive scale, and mathematical optimisation — gradient descent — adjusts billions of parameters to minimise prediction error. Intelligence emerges. It was never designed. It was trained.

1943–58

McCulloch-Pitts Neurons & Perceptrons

The biological metaphor: artificial neurons that fire or don't. Rosenblatt's perceptron learns from examples. Minsky and Papert kill the dream in 1969 by proving perceptrons cannot solve XOR. A twenty-year winter follows.

1986

Backpropagation — The Algorithm That Changes Everything

Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams demonstrate that error gradients can be propagated back through multi-layer networks, allowing deep learning. The mathematical key exists. Hardware is not yet ready.

2006–12

Deep Learning Renaissance

Hinton, LeCun, Bengio. GPUs make matrix multiplication fast enough. ImageNet competition results shock the computer vision community. The scaling hypothesis begins to crystallise: more data, more parameters, more compute — better results.

2017

"Attention Is All You Need" — The Transformer

Vaswani et al. at Google introduce the transformer architecture. Self-attention allows models to weigh relationships across entire sequences simultaneously. The architecture that powers every frontier language model is born.

2020–26

Large Language Models — Turing's Test Surpassed

GPT-3, GPT-4, Claude, Gemini. At sufficient scale — billions to trillions of parameters, trained on hundreds of billions of tokens — capabilities emerge that no one designed: reasoning, coding, literary analysis, scientific synthesis. An average interrogator in five minutes cannot reliably identify the machine. Turing's wager, paid out twenty years late and by a completely unexpected method.

The Turing Path (Failed)

  • Explicit rule encoding
  • Hand-crafted knowledge bases
  • Symbolic logical inference
  • Human experts as bottleneck
  • Brittle under ambiguity
  • Ceiling: narrow domains only

The Neural Path (Succeeded)

  • No explicit rules whatsoever
  • Statistical patterns from data
  • Gradient descent optimisation
  • Scale as the primary lever
  • Robust across ambiguity
  • Emergent general capability

The irony Turing could not have foreseen: the successful machine is not a Turing machine in any meaningful sense. It does not compute in the formal, rule-following way he theorised. It is, in architecture, closer to a brain — a vast web of weighted connections shaped by experience — than to anything he imagined when he wrote his 1950 paper.

The Wager, Settled

Turing predicted a behavioural outcome. He was right. He predicted a timeline. He was twenty years optimistic. He predicted a mechanism. He was entirely wrong. And in a final vindication of his softer prediction — that educated opinion would shift — we now speak of machines reasoning, understanding, and hallucinating without expecting philosophical challenge.

He asked the right question, set the right test, and misidentified the path. The destination vindicated the prophet; the journey surprised him completely.

✍️ Basic Generative AI: The Foundation Foundation

▼

Your thinking partner for discussing, interpreting, and assessing ideas — drawing on vast knowledge compressed into trained weights to help you explore a topic, weigh arguments, and sharpen your own thinking.

▶️ See How it Works

When you want to think through a topic — explore its tensions, weigh competing interpretations, draft discussion questions, or assess the strength of an argument — generative AI is a brilliant, tireless interlocutor. During training it absorbed an enormous breadth of knowledge, compressing billions of associations into mathematical weights. Your prompt activates the relevant patterns in those weights; generation then proceeds token by token from that activation. No documents are retrieved. No search is performed. The "library" was dissolved into cognition at training time — vast and richly interconnected, but frozen at a cutoff date and unable to cite a specific source.

Example — Generative AI as thinking partner

"What are the strongest arguments for and against crediting Rosalind Franklin as a co-discoverer of the DNA double helix?" — the AI engages the intellectual and ethical dimensions, weighs the evidence, and helps you think through the question, drawing on compressed training associations.

Where Generative AI reaches its limit

"What does the latest scholarship say about Franklin's role, and where can participants read more?" — for that question, you need RAG. Generative AI thinks with you from what it already knows; RAG harvests specific, current, citable sources and generates from them.

⚡ RAG: Harvest · Repository · Mine Retrieval

▼

RAG solves the same problem as generative AI — how to respond usefully to your question — but by a different route: it first harvests relevant passages from a document index, assembles them into a targeted repository in the context window, then mines that repository using the same token-by-token generation process.

The three phases are distinct and sequential — retrieval happens before generation begins, not during it:

Phase 1 — Harvest

Your prompt is encoded as a vector and matched against pre-indexed documents — uploaded readings, web pages, transcripts, syllabi. The most relevant passages are selected by similarity. Not everything: only what the search judges pertinent. Like a student surveying the library stacks and pulling the relevant volumes.

Phase 2 — Repository

The harvested passages are assembled into the context window alongside your original prompt. This is the "spreading sources on the desk" step. The language model has not yet begun to write — it simply now has specific, curated material in front of it. The sources remain intact and citable.

Phase 3 — Mine

Generation proceeds token by token, exactly as in basic generative AI — but now conditioned on both your prompt and the harvested repository. The response is grounded in specific documents and can cite page references or links precisely. The same writing process; a different desk.

Example — RAG applied to course preparation

"Based on this week's assigned reading on Rosalind Franklin, what three discussion questions would probe the most contested interpretations?" — a RAG system harvests the relevant passages from the uploaded text, assembles them as context, and mines them to craft questions grounded in specific arguments and evidence, with page references.

💡 Two Architectures, One Problem Synthesis

▼

Both architectures solve the same fundamental problem: all possible knowledge is too large to reason over simultaneously. They solve it at different moments in time — and that difference shapes everything.

Generative AI solves it at training time — compressing billions of associations into weights so that your prompt activates the relevant patterns without any runtime search. The library was dissolved into cognition during training. It cannot be searched or cited, but its associations are vast and richly interconnected, and it is always ready to think with you.

RAG solves it at runtime — harvesting relevant passages from a live index, assembling them into the context window, and generating from that curated repository. The sources remain intact, citable, and verifiable. The index can be updated with new documents at any time.

Think of it this way: the generative scholar has read everything and absorbed it — ask them anything and they reason from deep, compressed knowledge. The RAG scholar goes to the library first, spreads the relevant sources on the desk, and writes from what is in front of them. Both write token by token. What differs is what they are writing from.

🎓 What This Means for BOLLI Course Work Application

▼

The harvest → repository → mine architecture makes RAG a precise and powerful tool for working with your actual course materials. When you upload a syllabus, a reading, or a session transcript and ask a RAG system a question about it, the system does not search its training memory — it harvests the relevant passages from your document, assembles them into a working context, and generates a response grounded specifically in what you gave it. Every claim is traceable back to a source passage.

This is the practical difference between asking a knowledgeable colleague what they remember about a topic and asking them to work through a stack of documents with you. Both are valuable. Only one is verifiable. For BOLLI's intellectually rigorous learners, that distinction matters: discussion should be grounded in evidence, not in an AI's impressions of what it once learned.

Generative AI, meanwhile, remains the right tool whenever the task is collaborative thinking — exploring a theme, weighing an argument, drafting discussion questions, or assessing competing interpretations. Its compressed associations are vast and always available. It thinks with you from what it already knows.

  • Harvest from your materials: Semantic search identifies the most relevant passages from syllabi, readings, and evaluations — selectivity is built into the process, not left to chance
  • Repository assembled for your question: The context window is populated with exactly what bears on your query — not everything, the right things — before generation begins
  • Mine with confidence: Generation proceeds from that grounded repository — responses cite sources, reduce hallucination, and remain traceable to specific passages
  • Index stays current: New documents can be added to the retrieval index at any time — unlike trained weights, the repository grows with your course
  • Cross-disciplinary reach: Meaning-based semantic search bridges BOLLI's interdisciplinary range — harvesting across history, literature, science, and social studies in a single query
  • Generative AI for thinking, RAG for sourcing: The best course preparation uses both — generative AI to deepen understanding, RAG to ground discussion in what specific documents actually say

The Bottom Line: Both generative AI and RAG generate token by token. What differs is what they are generating from. Generative AI generates from vast compressed associations activated by your prompt — brilliant for thinking together, unable to cite a source. RAG generates from a curated repository harvested from your specific documents — grounded, citable, and verifiable. Your prompt is the funnel: in generative AI it funnels probability; in RAG it funnels documents.

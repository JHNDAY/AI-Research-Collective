Nonpolar Aliphatic

The hydrophobic core builders. Each carries a pure hydrocarbon R-group with no polar atoms. The tetrahedral geometry of the α-carbon is clearly visible in 3D, and the branched-chain members (Val, Leu, Ile) show how side-chain bulk increases with carbon count. Methionine is the lone sulfur-bearing member, its thioether capping an unbranched four-carbon chain.

Hydrophobic · Aromatic
Aromatic

Flat, electron-rich ring systems that absorb UV light and pack tightly in protein cores. Phenylalanine has a simple benzene ring. Tryptophan carries the largest side chain of any standard amino acid — a bicyclic indole with an NH visible as a blue nitrogen. Tyrosine is phenylalanine with a para-hydroxyl, making it ionizable at high pH.

Polar Neutral

Hydrogen-bond donors and acceptors that are uncharged at physiological pH. Asparagine and Glutamine each carry an amide group at the chain tip — visible as a blue nitrogen flanked by a red oxygen. Cysteine's large yellow sulfur marks the thiol that forms disulfide bridges. Serine and Threonine bear hydroxyl groups; Threonine gains a methyl, giving it two chiral centers.

Charged · Acidic
Acidic

Negatively charged at physiological pH — each carries a second carboxyl group on the side chain with a pKa well below 7. Both show four oxygens total (two per –COOH), rendered with double-bond cylinders. Aspartic acid has a single –CH₂– spacer between α-carbon and side-chain carboxyl; Glutamic acid adds one more carbon, giving it a slightly higher pKa and more conformational reach.

Charged · Basic
Basic

Positively charged at physiological pH — each carries a nitrogen-rich side chain. Arginine's guanidinium group has three nitrogens in a flat Y-shaped arrangement, making it the most basic amino acid (pKa ≈ 12.5). Histidine's imidazole ring sits near neutrality (pKa ≈ 6.0), making it a key catalytic residue in enzymes. Lysine has a simple four-carbon chain tipped with a freely accessible –NH₂.

Structurally Exceptional
Unique

Two amino acids that break the rules of the standard blueprint. Glycine's α-carbon carries two identical hydrogens — making it the only achiral amino acid. Rotate end-on to see the perfect symmetry. Proline's nitrogen is locked into a five-membered pyrrolidine ring, making it a secondary amine with no free –NH₂. This rigid geometry forces a kink in any polypeptide chain, making proline a helix-breaker in protein structure.

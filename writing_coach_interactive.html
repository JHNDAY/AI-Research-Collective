The Writing Coach

Bridging the Gap Between Human Thought and Machine Understanding

The Core Problem

When writers engage with AI systems, they face a fundamental paradox: the machine can only respond to what is explicitly written, yet writers often assume the machine somehow understands the broader context of their thinking—the background knowledge they possess, the nuances they've considered, the boundaries they assume are obvious. This gap between what is said and what is known creates incomplete prompts that yield incomplete responses. The Writing Coach solves this through structured iteration and dialogue, making implicit knowledge explicit and transforming solitary writing into collaborative discovery.

Issue #1: The Incompleteness Gap

The Problem: What the Writer Assumes the Machine Knows

The fundamental challenge in human-to-machine communication is what we might call assumption collapse. A writer thinks about their topic continuously—in the shower, during morning coffee, while running. They've accumulated layers of context, half-formed connections, previous conversations, and implicit constraints. When they finally sit down to write a prompt, they've compressed months of thinking into a few sentences. The writer assumes the AI system has access to this background, but it doesn't.

⚠ The Transcription Problem

Typical scenario: A retired engineer writes: "I want to explore AI and creativity." This seems clear to them because they've been thinking about whether machine-generated art constitutes real creativity, whether they value it differently from human-made work, and how this relates to their own sense of creative identity in retirement.

What the AI receives: Seven words with no emotional stakes, no personal context, no boundary markers, no indication of what "explore" means (brainstorm? debate? analyze philosophically?).

Result: Generic response that misses the person entirely.

Why This Happens

  • Egocentric bias: The writer knows their own thinking so intimately that they forget it's not publicly visible in their written words.
  • Context compression: Months of thought become sentences. The richness gets lost in translation.
  • Uncertainty about what matters: Writers often don't know which details are essential and which are noise, so they omit specifics that turn out to be crucial.
  • Fear of over-explanation: Especially true for educated adults; there's a hesitation to state the obvious, which isn't obvious at all to the machine.
  • Invisible scaffolding: The writer's prior conversations, readings, and arguments exist in their memory but nowhere in the prompt itself.

The Writing Coach's Solution: Structured Extraction Through Dialogue

The Writing Coach doesn't solve this by asking the writer to produce a perfect prompt on the first try. Instead, it makes incompleteness visible and converts it into a resource for improvement.

✓ How the Writing Coach Addresses Incomplete Transcription

Step 1 — Collaborative Review: The writer drafts a narrative prompt in the Writing Coach interface. This becomes their first articulation of the topic.

Step 2 — Targeted Feedback: Claude (in the role of BOLLI Writing Coach) provides feedback in three concrete parts:

  • Two specific things working well (showing what got through clearly)
  • One concrete suggestion (usually: "Add more personal context," "Be clearer about what you want to learn," or "Reveal more about your relationship to this topic")
  • A note on visualization (what themes would emerge if this were visualized)

Step 3 — Iterative Completion: The writer revises based on this feedback, making their implicit knowledge explicit. Each iteration fills in more of the gap.

Key Insight: The Writing Coach doesn't assume the first draft is wrong—it assumes the first draft is incomplete by design. The feedback process is a structured way to make the invisible scaffolding visible. By asking "what do you actually want to learn?" or "what's your stake in this?", the coach forces the writer to transcribe the thinking they didn't know they had to transcribe.

Concrete Example of Transcription Improvement

Draft 1 (Incomplete):

"I want to learn about AI and misinformation. How can I think more clearly about which sources to trust?"

Coaching Feedback:

This is clear in intention, but the coach asks: "You mention thinking more clearly—what prompted this question? Have you encountered misinformation personally? What would change if you had a better framework?"

Draft 2 (More Complete):

"I'm a retired librarian, and I'm disturbed by how easily I'm fooled by AI-generated content that looks authentic. I want to develop a personal framework for source evaluation in an era where I can't trust visual or textual 'authenticity' anymore. My stake: I care about passing reliable epistemic habits to my grandchildren."

Notice how Draft 2 contains: professional identity, emotional trigger, specific goal, personal stakes, intergenerational dimension. The machine now has material to work with.

Issue #2: The Discovery Dimension

The Problem: What the Writer Doesn't Yet Know They're Thinking

Writers come to AI systems with what they think they want to explore. But the act of writing and revision often reveals new questions, dimensions, and implications that weren't in the initial framing. The danger is that a clumsy first prompt can lead to answers that don't open up these new territories—they merely reinforce what the writer already expected to think.

⚠ The Discovery Problem

Typical scenario: A retired mathematician writes: "I want to understand whether AI can truly reason, or if it's just pattern matching."

The initial answer: Probably a well-articulated response to the binary question posed—and the writer nods along, satisfied. Discussion ends.

What got lost: The opportunity to discover that the very distinction between "true reasoning" and "pattern matching" might be a false dichotomy. That human reasoning is sophisticated pattern matching. That the interesting question might actually be about kinds of reasoning, not existence vs. illusion.

Why Vague Prompts Prevent Discovery

  • Narrow framing: Generic prompts invite generic responses that fit neatly into existing categories.
  • Assumption confirmation: When a prompt is vague, the AI often produces something the writer already suspected, reinforcing rather than expanding their thinking.
  • Missing context clues: Without knowing who is asking and why, the AI can't identify the hidden assumptions embedded in the question itself.
  • Lost personalization: Discovery is personal. A generic response about "reasoning" won't spark anything for a mathematician; a response that connects to how they think might crack open new territory.

The Writing Coach's Solution: Specificity as a Gateway to Emergence

The Writing Coach creates conditions for discovery by moving from generic questions to specific narratives. This specificity doesn't constrain the AI's response—it expands it by giving the AI something real to work with.

✓ How the Writing Coach Enables Discovery

The mechanism: When a writer includes personal stakes, professional background, and specific examples in their narrative prompt, the AI's response becomes contextual. Instead of answering an abstract question, it's engaging with a particular human's particular thinking. This opens new dimensions.

The iteration effect: As the writer refines their narrative through coaching feedback, they often discover while writing that they care about something different than they initially thought. The revision process itself is discovery. The coach's questions ("What's your stake in this?" "What would change?") are invitations to surprise yourself.

Key Insight: Specificity is not a constraint on AI—it's a catalyst for emergence. When the AI knows who is asking and why, it can identify the actual question beneath the surface question. It can spot the hidden assumptions and gently disturb them. It can offer not just answers but perspective shifts.

How These Issues Interweave

Incompleteness and discovery are not separate problems—they're two faces of the same phenomenon: the translation problem between human tacit knowledge and machine-interpretable text.

Gap 1 — The Transcription Gap: What humans think is not automatically what they write. Writing reveals incompleteness. The Writing Coach makes this incompleteness productive by asking: "What are you assuming I know? What's your stake in this question? What would change if you succeeded?"
Gap 2 — The Discovery Gap: Generic questions invite generic answers. Specific, embodied narratives invite perspective shifts and emergent thinking. The Writing Coach creates conditions for the writer to surprise themselves and for the AI to offer genuine insight rather than safe generalization.
Gap 3 — The Artifact Gap: Thinking that exists only in text feels ephemeral. Visualizing the response in HTML makes the dialogue concrete, shareable, and memorable. It transforms a conversation into a creation.

The Three-Step Value Chain

1. Making Implicit Knowledge Explicit

The coaching feedback forces writers to articulate what they've been assuming without knowing they were assuming it. "What's your stake in this?" surfaces the emotional and intellectual weight of the question.

2. Creating Specific Narratives

Specificity is not about limiting the AI's response—it's about giving it real ground to stand on. A narrative that includes identity, stakes, and embodied knowledge invites the AI to engage with a particular human rather than an abstract question.

3. Enabling Emergence Through Response Visualization

Converting the refined prompt + AI response into a polished HTML artifact concretizes the thinking and makes patterns visible that were implicit in the text.

Why This Matters for Retired Adult Learners

The Writing Coach emerges from BOLLI's mission to serve retired adult learners aged 60–85. This population brings unique assets and challenges to AI literacy:

Assets

  • Embodied expertise: Decades of professional experience and deep knowledge in specific domains.
  • Reflective capacity: Time and disposition for thoughtful engagement with ideas.
  • Clear stakes: Questions often emerge from genuine life challenges and intellectual curiosities.
  • Narrative sophistication: Ability to craft nuanced stories and articulate complex ideas.

Challenges

  • Technical friction: The interface and process of "prompting" can feel alien and unintuitive.
  • Assumption about "knowing it all": A lifetime of expertise can make it harder to articulate fundamentals to a machine.
  • First-draft assumptions: The tendency to assume a prompt is good enough because the writer understands it perfectly.

The Writing Coach solves these by:

Translating embodied expertise into machine-interpretable narratives through a warm, feedback-based process that treats incomplete first drafts as a feature, not a bug. It says: "Your thinking is sophisticated—now let's make it explicit so the machine can engage with your actual questions, not a flat version of them."

The Writing Coach's Real Innovation

The Writing Coach doesn't improve prompting by teaching people to write better prompts. It improves prompting by recognizing that human thinking is tacit, embodied, and emergent—and creating a structured process to make it explicit, specific, and discoverable. It treats the gap between human knowledge and machine interpretation not as an engineering problem but as a creative opportunity.

"The goal is not to engineer the perfect prompt from the start. The goal is to use dialogue—with a coach and with an AI—to discover what you actually want to know."

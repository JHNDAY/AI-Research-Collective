AI in Education — Are Students Using It?
Beth Mazer opens with her Northwestern granddaughter who refuses AI for papers. Ronald Marcus counters with the "Index Medicus" parallel — AI as a dramatically faster research index. The group lands on "starting point, not ending point."

7–9 min
A Publisher Pulls an AI-Written Book
Speaker 5 describes a UK book pulled after reviewers flagged "clunky metaphors" and repetitive paragraphs. Alan Nemeth raises: should quality — not AI authorship alone — determine whether a book is published? Contracts are already requiring human authorship.
publishingauthorshipIP
9–20 min
The Optimistic View — Will AI Free Us for Better Work?
Ollie frames the Robert Reich position: technology historically automates drudgery, freeing workers for more cognitive roles. Peter Shapiro (ex-consultant) and Lois Sockol (ex-teacher) apply this to their own professions — finding nuance around the emotional and relational dimensions of work that resist automation.

18–26 min
What Jobs Can AI Really Replace? The Relational Dimension
Alan Nemeth challenges the "AI as data replacement" framing — consulting is industrial psychology, not fact delivery. The group probes: Can AI replicate human connection, empathy, and political judgment in meetings? A tentative consensus: complex interpersonal roles remain human. For now.

Real Layoffs Now — Amazon, Meta, Block
Ollie presents data: Block laid off 40% of staff (stock +20%). Meta planning 15,000 jobs cut. Amazon's warehouses now 50% robotic. Entry-level office workers, legal/medical secretaries, payroll clerks: 3–4 million at high risk. Truck drivers: 2 million. Software devs: 1.6 million. "A million here, a million there…"

Agentic AI — The Shift Nobody Has Seen Yet
Ollie introduces agentic computing: AI that acts, not just answers. Mark's son runs a startup where 4 people now do the work of 10, with one engineer communicating with Claude more than with his manager. The group grapples with whether agents could coordinate, manage, and replace middle layers.

The Dire Warnings — Hinton & Amodei
Jeff Hinton: AI will match or surpass human cognitive capabilities in 5–20 years. Dario Amodei (Anthropic CEO): 15–25% unemployment in 1–5 years. Ford CEO Farley: AI will wipe out half of white-collar jobs. "If AI can do any job people can do, retraining becomes meaningless."

63–80 min
The T-Shirt Conglomerate — Post-Scarcity Thought Experiment
Ollie proposes a fully automated t-shirt company — cotton grown, spun, distributed by robots. Cost: $1. If this happens across the economy, would prices plummet into abundance? Sam Altman's vision. Counter: If nobody works, nobody earns money to buy anything. Great Depression parallel. Henry Ford rebuttal: pay workers, expand the market.

The Safety Net — Who Pays? How?
Bob Mahoney: regulate layoffs, force companies to fund unemployment. Tax excess AI profits. Tax data center compute (gigawatts). Bernie Sanders: stronger unions. Ronald Marcus: a "vicious circle" — the dollar shirt requires taxing the dollar shirt. Peter Shapiro: historically it takes a crisis to force change. WWII solved the Depression. What's next?

Closing: Optimism, Value Shifts & The 15-Hour Week

Group Tone
Predominantly pragmatic-concerned — accepting AI's trajectory as real and accelerating, but anxious about velocity, distribution of harm, and the absence of any governing framework. The tone grew more sobered as data accumulated. Moments of genuine optimism (Alan Nemeth, Marie Danziger) were present but outnumbered.
Voices in the Room
Participant Profiles — Role & Stance
Ollie
Facilitator · AI Course Instructor
Neutral / Guiding
"We're all speculating, so we don't know the answer. The future will tell us." Balanced framing throughout, surfacing both optimistic and dire scenarios without advocacy.
Lois Sockol
Former Teacher · Most Skeptical Voice
Resistant — Fighting Back
"I am in constant battle with AI. And so far, I want you to know, I'm holding my own." Personally wrestles weekly with "Happy," the AI in her memoir app. Questioned whether emotional teaching can ever be automated.
Beth Mazer
Participant · Accuracy Skeptic
Cautiously Skeptical
"I think it may be a question of not trusting what you're getting at a certain level." Opened the session with a story of her granddaughter rejecting AI — her instinct is that accuracy concerns limit usefulness for serious work.
Ronald Marcus
Physician (Retired) · Pragmatist
Pragmatic — Useful Tool
"You go to AI, it tells you all the references, and you're done in 3 minutes. This used to take us literally hours." The Index Medicus analogy — AI as dramatically faster research infrastructure — was his anchor. Later troubled by the vicious circle of taxing AI profits.
Peter Shapiro
Management Consultant (Retired)
Analytical / Darkening
"One can easily go down a spiral of despair on this… what the hell are we going to do with 8 billion people?" Noted AI-washing as a real phenomenon. Raised the WWII-as-Depression-solution parallel as a disturbing historical template.
Bob Mahoney
Participant · Safety Net Advocate
Resigned — Proactive
"We're on a track toward more unemployment… there must be more talk about the safety net before it's too late." Most focused on policy solutions: mandatory unemployment contributions from companies that use AI to lay off workers.
Alan Nemeth
Participant · Constructive Optimist
Cautiously Optimistic
"What we can be doing with this powerful technology is focus on improvement in quality — not use it to get rid of people's labor." Pushed for valuing trades and vocational work. His grandsons: an Outward Bound guide and a soon-to-be teacher.
Margo Goldman
Participant · Complexity Theorist
Cynical / Systemic
"We have capitalism, and we have greed… there's no guarantee that if the cost of goods goes down, they're going to lower the price." Introduced complexity theory and climate change as unpredictable forces disrupting any neat AI-abundance scenario.
Mark (Speaker 7)
Participant · Economic Historian
Analytically Balanced
"Henry Ford's principle was: implement efficient manufacturing, pay your workers well, and they will buy your cars." Offered European redistribution models and the New Deal pendulum as historical precedents. His son's startup is already running 4 people at the output of 10.
Barbara Kriss
Participant · Velocity Worrier
Worried — About Speed
"The wild card is just the velocity at which all this is happening, and whether we can evolve fast enough to adjust." Asked the sharpest question: even if the right policies exist, can democratic institutions move fast enough?

The Problem

Three Ways AI Undermines
Clinical Training

1
Risk One
Deskilling

Established clinicians losing previously acquired skills through overreliance on AI systems for tasks they once performed themselves.

2
Risk Two
Mis-skilling

Learning the wrong things from inaccurate AI systems — internalizing flawed reasoning patterns as if they were valid clinical knowledge.

3
Risk Three · Most Consequential
Never-skilling

Trainees who never acquire the cognitive foundations of clinical expertise because AI supplied the answers before the struggle that produces mastery could occur.

How Expertise Develops

Three Essential Requirements
for Mastery

Drawing on Master Adaptive Learning Theory, Knowledge Encapsulation, and Dual Process Theory, the authors identify convergent requirements — each threatened by AI.

Requirement 01
Effortful Engagement with Authentic Problems

The learner must do the cognitive work themselves. A resident puzzling through why a patient with cirrhosis has low platelets — reasoning from portal hypertension → splenomegaly → splenic sequestration — reorganizes knowledge and builds pattern recognition through that effortful processing.

AI Threat When AI generates a differential diagnosis, the trainee receives an answer without the cognitive struggle that reorganizes knowledge.
Requirement 02
Accumulated Experience Over Time

There are no shortcuts to expertise. Pattern libraries at the basis of expert intuition form over thousands of hours — hundreds of cases processed slowly before they become automatic. Each case adds to the learner's library and refines their illness scripts.

AI Threat AI interrupts the hypothesis-testing feedback loops that learners must cycle through hundreds of times before clinical reasoning becomes automatic.
Requirement 03
Metacognitive Awareness

Experts don't just know more — they know what they know and what they don't know. They recognize when a situation is within their competence vs. when to slow down, seek help, or gather more information.

AI Threat AI tools provide answers with uniform, sycophantic confidence, preventing learners from calibrating their own sense of reliability across different clinical situations.
AI has the potential to accelerate learning, but only if designed to stimulate reasoning rather than replace it — to serve as coach rather than crutch.

The Path Forward

Seven Design & Implementation
Strategies

🔬
Strategy 01
AI as Expert Consultant

Implement commit-then-compare protocols: trainees must commit to a differential diagnosis, workup, and treatment plan before seeing AI suggestions — mirroring how trainees present to attendings before orders are written.

Commit → Compare → Learn
🧠
Strategy 02
AI as Coach

Design AI tools to require engagement, not just provide answers. Ask questions like "What's the most dangerous diagnosis to rule out?" — shifting AI from answer-provider to reasoning coach. Enable AI-guided simulation and tailored assessments.

Socratic, Not Oracular
🛡️
Strategy 03
AI as Guardian Angel

Use AI to identify important issues the trainee overlooked, reduce cognitive biases, and prevent errors — without replacing the initial reasoning process that must occur first.

Safety Net, Not Scaffold
🚫
Strategy 04
AI-Free Zones

Designate learning environments — morning report, case conferences, bedside rounds — as AI-free zones. Trainees reason from their own knowledge, giving faculty the ability to assess developing stand-alone competence.

Preserve Unassisted Reasoning
📈
Strategy 05
Incremental AI Access

Stage AI availability across training: early trainees have limited access, increasing as foundational competencies are demonstrated. Minimum case volumes without AI — analogous to procedure volume requirements for technical competence.

Earned Access Model
📊
Strategy 06
AI to Support Metacognition

Record trainees' assessments before AI consultation, track reasoning accuracy over time, and prompt trainees to estimate confidence. Metacognitive awareness — how well confidence correlates with accuracy — can be monitored and fed back.

Track · Calibrate · Improve
📚
Strategy 07
AI Education for All

Trainees must understand AI strengths, weaknesses, and failure modes — including training data biases and error types. Attending physicians need to learn to supervise in AI-augmented environments: probing whether trainees truly understand what they present or are merely relaying AI output, modeling appropriate AI skepticism.

Literacy for Trainees and Supervisors Alike

Core Argument

The most consequential risk AI poses to medicine is not deskilling seasoned clinicians but never-skilling trainees — failing to build the cognitive foundations of expertise because AI answers arrived before the productive struggle could occur. Clinical mastery requires effortful engagement, accumulated experience, and metacognitive calibration. AI threatens all three. Thoughtful design — AI as coach, not crutch — can harness AI's potential for precision education while preserving the conditions under which genuine expertise develops. These recommendations are reasoned from theory; the authors call for urgent empirical research to test which interventions truly work.

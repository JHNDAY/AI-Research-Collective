AI Elementsof Style
Guiding Principles for the Design of Discussion Visualizations
After William Strunk Jr. & E.B. White — The Elements of Style (1959)  ·  Adapted for Visual Communication with Artificial Intelligence  ·  2026
First Principle
I
Omit Needless Elements
Every element in a visualization must earn its place. If it does not inform, guide, or invite — remove it. Decoration is a failure of discipline.
Second Principle
II
Prefer the Specific
A vague headline announces nothing. A sharp question opens a room. Name the issue exactly. Specificity is respect for the reader's intelligence.
Third Principle
III
Restraint Is Force
Emptiness becomes eloquence. The designer who knows what to remove is more powerful than the one who knows what to add. White space is argument.
The Canon
Seven Rules
Rule 1
Make the paragraph the unit of composition
Strunk: "The paragraph is the unit of composition." In visualization: the panel is the unit. Each panel carries one idea, one question, one dimension of the issue. Never cram two arguments into one panel.
One panel, one idea. Full stop.
Rule 2
Use the active voice
Passive construction buries agency. "The policy was contested" hides who contested it. In visualization: name the actor, the action, the consequence. Passive data is inert. Active framing invites argument.
Name the actor. State the action. Invite the dispute.
Rule 3
Put statements in positive form
Strunk: "Make definite assertions." Hedged labels and qualified headlines train readers to discount them. State what the data shows, not what it fails to disprove. Uncertainty can be named — but name it directly, not by evasion.
Assert. Do not hedge into invisibility.
Rule 4
Omit needless words
"Vigorous writing is concise." The same law governs labels, captions, and headlines. If three words do the work of eight, use three. The reader's attention is finite. Spend it on signal, not scaffolding.
Cut by half. Then cut again.
Rule 5
Avoid a succession of loose sentences
A visualization that strings together disconnected data points without hierarchy has no spine. Establish the main claim first. Supporting detail follows. Drill-down comes last. Order is argument.
Hierarchy first. Everything else descends from it.
Rule 6
Keep related words together
Strunk's rule of proximity. In visualization: group what belongs together. Separate what is distinct. The eye reads proximity as relationship. If two elements sit together, the viewer assumes they're connected. Use that assumption deliberately.
Proximity is meaning. Group with intention.
Rule 7
Express coordinate ideas in similar form
Parallel structure signals parallel importance. If three trends are presented, treat them identically — same weight, same format, same visual grammar. Inconsistency implies hierarchy where none was intended. Parallel ideas demand parallel treatment.
Same structure = same status. Break it deliberately or not at all.
Applied Standards
The One-Pager
The One-Pager Protocol

Every discussion visualization conforms to this structure and no other:

1. The Issue — one sentence, active voice, specific
2. The Core Question — what the group is being asked to consider
3. The Evidence — the minimum data required; no more
4. The Drill-Down — available; never forced; clearly signaled

Engagement Principle
Context, Not Authority
Framing Law
Offer the landscape — do not lecture from it
The systematic view opens the conversation. It does not close it. Present the temperature of the news as a threshold the group may step through — not as the answer they are being handed.
Participants chose to engage. They were not pushed.
Agency Law
Drill-down is an offer, not an instruction
The participant reaches for depth. The designer does not push it. A drill-down that requires activation is evidence of respect. A visualization that opens at full depth is an ambush.
Signal the door. Do not push them through it.

Banned Elements

If you violate: remove the element. Do not explain its presence. Correction is silent revision.

Before You Publish
Pre-Release Checklist
  • Does each panel carry exactly one idea?
  • Is the headline in active voice, specific, assertive?
  • Has every decorative element been removed?
  • Does the hierarchy read without instruction?
  • Is the core question visible within 3 seconds?
  • Is drill-down available but not forced?
  • Would removing anything break comprehension? (If not, remove it.)
  • Does it invite argument — or foreclose it?
  • Any wrong → revise before publishing
Correction Protocol
When It Fails
  • Watch where participants actually drill — not where you assumed they would
  • If they gloss over a panel, it carried the wrong question or too many
  • If discussion stalls, the frame was too closed — open the question
  • Revise silently. Apply correction to future work. Do not explain the old version.

The Radio Corollary
Murrow's Law Applied
Broadcast Standard
You cannot overload the ear — and you cannot overload the eye
Edward R. Murrow understood that radio permitted no revision, no re-reading, no second chance. Every word had to arrive at full weight on the first transmission. Visualization works under the same constraint: the viewer reads once, in real time, in the middle of a conversation. If it does not land immediately, it does not land at all.
Design for first contact. There is no second reading.
Invisible Guidance
The hierarchy must be so clear it disappears
When structure is visible, it calls attention to itself and away from the content. The goal is a hierarchy so obvious the viewer does not perceive they have been guided — only that they understood. Craft that works leaves no fingerprints.
If they see the design, it failed. If they see the idea, it succeeded.
Memory & Continuity
Working with AI Review
Reference Document Protocol

This document is a standing contract between the designer and the AI reviewer. When requesting visualization review:

1. Attach this document to the conversation
2. State: "Review against the AI Elements of Style"
3. The reviewer applies these rules explicitly, not from memory

AI memory has recency bias. This document eliminates dependency on retrieval.

The Turing Principle
Pragmatic Standard
If it works, use it — and ask why later
The test of a visualization is behavior, not theory. Does it open discussion? Does it invite argument? Does it orient without overwhelming? If yes: ship it. The mechanism behind its success is secondary. The result is primary.
Utility is the measure. Everything else is philosophy.

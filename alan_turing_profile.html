1936
On Computable Numbers
In a single 1936 paper, Turing invented the theoretical computer before any electronic computer existed. His abstract "Turing Machine" — a read/write head moving over an infinite tape — defined the mathematical limits of computation itself, answering Hilbert's Entscheidungsproblem in the negative: no algorithm can decide all mathematical truths.
I
1936
The Halting Problem
Turing proved there are problems no computer — regardless of speed or memory — can ever solve. By showing no program can reliably determine if another program will halt or run forever, he drew the boundary between the computable and the unknowable, founding theoretical computer science as a discipline.
II
1945
The ACE Computer Design
Turing's design for the Automatic Computing Engine at the National Physical Laboratory was among the first detailed blueprints for a stored-program electronic computer — a machine that holds its own instructions in memory, the architecture underlying every computer built since. His design was technically ahead of its institutional surroundings.
III
1952
Morphogenesis & Reaction-Diffusion
In a stunning departure, Turing published a mathematical model explaining how biological patterns — leopard spots, zebra stripes, embryonic differentiation — arise from uniform chemical systems through instability. His reaction-diffusion equations became foundational to mathematical biology and are still actively validated in labs today.

1950
Computing Machinery and Intelligence — The Imitation Game
"The original question, 'Can machines think?' I believe to be too meaningless to deserve discussion."In what may be the most cited paper in artificial intelligence, Turing asked "Can machines think?" — and immediately reframed it as an operational question. His Imitation Game proposed a behavioral test for machine intelligence that re-oriented the entire field: intelligence is not a metaphysical property but a functional capacity. The paper anticipated objections from theology to mathematics, dismantling each in turn with characteristic wit. It launched not one but three research programs: symbolic AI, machine learning, and philosophy of mind.

II. The War Hero

Bletchley Park & the Breaking of Enigma

When Britain declared war in September 1939, Turing walked through the gates of Bletchley Park as one of its first civilian recruits. The German Enigma machine — with its astronomical number of possible settings — was encrypting virtually every Wehrmacht, Kriegsmarine, and Luftwaffe transmission. The Polish Biuro Szyfrów had made the initial break; the Germans then increased Enigma's complexity. Turing was handed the harder problem.

Turing took a mechanical predecessor and transformed it into something radically new: the Bombe, an electromechanical device that exploited cribs — known or guessed plaintext fragments — to eliminate impossible Enigma settings at machine speed. Where the Polish device needed known daily keys, Turing's Bombe needed only a likely phrase. It was a logical engine disguised as electrical engineering.

He then turned to the Naval Enigma — the hardest variant, used by U-boats hunting Allied convoys in the Atlantic. Turing's statistical technique, Banburismus, used overlapping coincidences in intercepted messages to narrow the search space. Working with seized codebooks from captured U-boats, his methods cracked naval communications and helped turn the Battle of the Atlantic.

Historian Sir Harry Hinsley estimated that Turing's work shortened the war in Europe by at least two to four years — and that without it, the outcome itself might have been different.

Turing received the OBE in 1945. The full nature of his contribution — and of Bletchley Park itself — remained classified until the 1970s. He was not knighted. He was never publicly celebrated in his lifetime for the act that may have saved millions of lives.

III. The Prophet

80 Years of Development He Saw Coming

Turing, 1950 → Now, 2025
The Imitation Game → Large Language Models
What Turing proposed
A machine that produces text indistinguishable from a human's in open-ended conversation. He predicted a machine could fool a human judge at least 30% of the time by the year 2000 — not through understanding, but through learned statistical behavior.
What we have now
GPT-4, Claude, Gemini — systems trained on billions of tokens that generate fluent text by predicting the next token. The architecture is wholly different from what Turing imagined; the behavioral outcome is precisely what he described. The Turing Test is now considered too easy a bar.
Turing, 1950 → Now, 2025
Child Machines → Machine Learning
What Turing proposed
Rather than programming adult intelligence directly, build a "child machine" and subject it to a teaching process. The machine should be rewarded for correct responses and penalized for wrong ones — modifying its internal state through experience rather than explicit rules.
What we have now
Reinforcement learning, backpropagation, RLHF. The training paradigm for every major AI system is structurally what Turing sketched: reward signals shaping internal representations. He was describing gradient descent 35 years before it was widely adopted.
Turing, 1948 → Now, 2025
Unorganised Machines → Neural Networks
What Turing proposed
In an unpublished 1948 report, Turing described "B-type unorganised machines" — networks of simple connected units whose connection weights could be modified by experience. The network's behavior would emerge from the pattern of connections, not explicit programming.
What we have now
Deep neural networks with billions of parameters. The transformer architecture underlying modern AI is precisely a large, differentiable network whose weights are updated by a learning signal. Turing did not predict the transformer — he predicted the paradigm it belongs to.
Turing, 1950 → Now, 2025
Objections to Machine Intelligence → AI Ethics & Alignment
What Turing proposed
Turing catalogued nine objections to thinking machines — theological, mathematical, consciousness-based, Lady Lovelace's objection — and answered each. He explicitly addressed the fear that machines might "take over," and the question of whether machine judgment could be trusted to act within sanctioned boundaries.
What we have now
The entire field of AI Safety and Alignment — ensuring AI systems behave as intended, remain under human control, and do not pursue unintended objectives. Every major lab now has an alignment team working on the questions Turing named in passing in a 14-page paper in 1950.
Turing, 1952 → Now, 2025
Morphogenesis → Computational Biology & Synthetic Patterning
What Turing proposed
Simple chemical reaction-diffusion systems — two interacting agents with different diffusion rates — can spontaneously produce complex spatial patterns. Biology's visible complexity might be generated by relatively simple mathematical rules operating across cells.
What we have now
Reaction-diffusion patterns have been confirmed in mouse digit spacing, fish stripe formation, and palate rugae development. Synthetic biologists use Turing's equations to engineer patterning in cells. His morphogenesis paper is more cited today than it was in his lifetime.
